Home / 3D head pose estimation

Intelligent Motion Lab · Jan 2024–Oct 2024

Stabilizing head pose in a robot loop where occlusion and geometry both matter.

The goal was not only to estimate head pose, but to make that estimate reliable enough to support real-time visual servoing on a UR5 setup with multiple cameras and endoscopes. The Spring 2024 computer vision report sharpened this work around clinical eye-exam scenarios, selective landmarks, and report-backed tracking evidence.

Report-backed pipeline

From selective landmarks to a stable control signal

The term project made the tracking stack much more explicit: treat head pose estimation as a filtering problem with a carefully chosen feature set, not just a detector output.

  • Used 3DDFA_V2 to detect four landmarks in each frame: the outer eye corners, the nose tip, and the nasion.
  • Defined an 8-value measurement space from 2D landmark coordinates and a 9-state UKF over translation, rotation, and inter-landmark distances.
  • Initialized the filter from BiWi pose annotations, then tuned process and measurement noise for 30 FPS head motion.
  • Carried the perception lessons into the larger robot setup with eye-to-hand and eye-in-hand calibration for two depth cameras and four endoscopes around a UR5 stack.

Dataset context

  • Validated on the BiWi Kinect head pose database.
  • 24 sequences from 20 subjects, recorded at roughly one meter from the sensor.
  • Presented as a UIUC computer vision term project focused on clinical eye-exam use cases.

Tracking takeaway

  • Left eye corner and nose tip were the most stable landmarks in practice.
  • Half-face poses created the largest spikes in error.
  • Frontal views were consistently the easiest frames to track.

Selected landmarks

Why the feature choice mattered

The report moved away from pupil-centered tracking and instead used the outer eye corners, nose tip, and nasion as a more stable feature set under partial occlusion and clinical motion.

Tracking evidence

What the report found

  • Average RMSE versus 3DDFA_V2 was 3.98 px for the right eye, 2.02 px for the left eye, 3.67 px for the nose tip, and 4.81 px for the nasion.
  • Centroid trajectories from UKF and 3DDFA_V2 stayed close even though the BiWi head-center annotation was biased below the chosen landmark centroid.
  • The strongest failure cases were half-face poses, where eye landmarks became harder to localize or confused in image space.

Engineering relevance

Why this mattered for the robot project

The report helped turn a perception idea into something more deployable: it showed where filtering genuinely stabilizes motion estimates and where the detector still breaks first.

  • The UKF layer showed that landmark filtering can smooth a pose signal enough to be useful before it reaches a visual servoing loop.
  • The results also clarified what to guard against in a robot-facing system: occlusion, low light, missed detections, and extreme half-face poses.
  • The main limitation was evaluation against 3DDFA_V2 rather than an external landmark ground truth, which the report identifies as the next step for stronger quantitative comparison.
