Journal paper

System design for telerehabilitation with minimal patient burden.

This is the only journal paper in the rehab thread, and it establishes the system-design workflow that the later papers build on.

Paper 1

Sensor optimization plus one-session PD adaptation

This paper does more than shrink the hardware. It optimizes the HAR system on healthy-subject data across 34 functional and LSVT-BIG activities, then adapts it to Parkinson's patients with only a single session of patient data.

Recall
95%
Adapted PD performance
>80%
  • The final deployable setup emphasized two sensors on the left thigh and left forearm with an NM classifier.
  • The paper reports healthy-subject precision, recall, and F1 above 90%, then above 80% for two PD subjects after one adaptation session.
  • The practical contribution is reducing patient burden without giving up a clinically usable recognition level.

Augmentation papers

Testing when synthetic rehab motion helps, and when it does not.

These papers approach augmentation from two angles: classification impact on a benchmark dataset and expert-scored realism for therapeutic motion.

Paper 2

Augmentation benchmarking on WISDM with ConvLSTM

This study is useful because it treats augmentation as something to benchmark, not assume. On the six-activity WISDM dataset, the model was retrained seven times to compare six augmentation methods against the raw baseline.

Raw
92%
Linear combination
93%
Scaling
97%
Jittering
98%
  • Jittering, scaling, and linear combination were the only methods that clearly improved test accuracy.
  • Stair activities remained the weak point for amplitude warping, time warping, and the autoencoder-based method.
  • All augmentations sped up training, but faster training did not automatically translate into better test performance.

Paper 3

Expert-scored realism for rehab exercise augmentation

This paper adds a qualitative layer that is often missing in HAR work: six-IMU signals for 21 therapeutic activities were visualized on a 3D body model and then scored by 18 experts for both similarity and variation.

Linear combination
4.84 / 5 similarity
Time warping
1.01 / 5 variation score
  • Across all 21 activities, time warping was the best overall choice, but not for every motion type.
  • For dynamic walking activities, time warping failed the 80% similarity threshold, so amplitude warping and linear combination were the safer choices.
  • Amplitude warping was preferred for stair down, PO-left, and standing posture, while time warping worked better for SFR-right and stair up.

Related extension

A modeling follow-up on simple and complex therapeutic motion.

This paper is not an augmentation study, but it extends the same rehab program into multi-task sequence modeling and stays relevant to the broader portfolio story.

Paper 4

Multi-task CNN-BiLSTM for simple vs. complex activities

The updated paper makes the setup clearer: 43 healthy subjects wore four smart bands on both wrists and thighs, and the model jointly handled 51 simple activities and 14 complex activities in a branched multi-task architecture.

CNN-LSTM
71.83% / 66.46%
CNN-BiLSTM
84.17% / 78.78%
  • The bidirectional model improved both simple and complex activity recognition, not just one branch.
  • The gains were especially meaningful for dynamic multi-phase motions like walking, stair climbing, and several LSVT-BIG activities.
  • The paper argues that future temporal context is a major advantage when activities contain multiple recurrent or staged phases.
