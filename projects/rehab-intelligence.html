A multi-year research thread on making wearable HAR practical for rehabilitation.

This case study bundles the work from my master's research and related publications into one narrative: reduce patient burden, understand which augmentations are actually useful, and design sequence models that can distinguish both simple and complex therapeutic activities.

Program scope Sensor placement, augmentation, and sequence modeling across multiple studies.

Paper 3

Qualitative validation of augmentation realism

This study used six IMUs, 21 therapeutic activities, a 3D human body model, and 18 expert raters to judge whether augmented signals still looked like real motion.

  • Time warping best preserved structure overall.
  • Amplitude warping helped specific activities like stairs and standing posture.
  • Walking remained the hardest regime to preserve convincingly.

Multi-task CNN-BiLSTM for simple and complex activities

A shared representation with deeper recurrent modeling improved recognition for both simple and complex activities compared with a CNN-LSTM alternative.

What ties the work together

A research arc from sensing constraints to model design

Across these papers, the pattern is consistent: start by reducing friction in data collection, build augmentation carefully enough to preserve semantics, and only then scale model complexity. That sequence is why this research thread feels cohesive rather than a disconnected set of experiments.
